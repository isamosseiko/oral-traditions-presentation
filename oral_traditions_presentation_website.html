What Is Oral Tradition?

Oral tradition is the practice of passing knowledge, history, and values through spoken stories rather than writing. Elders and storytellers protect these stories to keep culture alive.

  • Shared through generations
  • Protects history and identity
  • Builds community connection
  • Preserves Indigenous knowledge
01

Types of Indigenous Stories

Indigenous oral traditions use different story types to explain the world, teach lessons, and guide behaviour.

  • Creation Stories: How life and land began
  • Origin Stories: Where people came from
  • Trickster Stories: Learning through humour
02

Creation & Trickster Stories

These stories explain nature, human behaviour, and spiritual beliefs. In many Haida stories, Raven brings light to the world and shapes the land.

  • Respect for land and animals
  • Balance and responsibility
  • Learning from mistakes
  • Spiritual connection
03

How Stories Are Told

Stories are shared during ceremonies, gatherings, and family events. Although details may change, the main meaning stays strong.

  • Led by Elders
  • Use rhythm and repetition
  • Adapted for audiences
  • Often in traditional languages
04

Characters & Conflicts

Many oral stories take place in ancient times, when animals, humans, and spirits could communicate.

  • Trickster figures and spirits
  • Forests, oceans, sky worlds
  • Conflicts: pride, greed, curiosity
  • Resolution through balance
05

Why These Stories Matter

Oral traditions continue to teach respect, responsibility, and connection to nature. They help protect language and identity.

  • Build cultural pride
  • Encourage respect
  • Connect generations
  • Support reconciliation
06

Conclusion & Works Cited

Oral traditions are living systems of knowledge that connect people to their ancestors, land, and future generations.
